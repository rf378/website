Introduction¶

Background/Context¶

Yelp is a public US corporation founded in 2004. The company’s main service offering is the Yelp.com website and Yelp mobile app, which publishes reviews about businesses. The company also provides an online reservation system called Yelp reservations. Yelp works by allowing its online users to rank the quality of a business from a one to five-star rating system. The main function of Yelp is to provide users information about the quality of offerings from businesses in a particular area.

The data set that our team chose to work with is a subset of Yelp’s user data, reviews, and businesses. Our data set is diverse in its scope – information about reviews are collected from eleven metropolitan areas across four countries. In summary, our data collects information about 5.2 million user reviews and 174,000 businesses.

Our group feels that this project is important for a number of reasons. First, due to the pandemic consumers are increasingly relying on online reviews to assess the quality of business offerings (i.e. there is less in-person trials). People are relying more on the infomration they collect from the internet. Second, ratings can affect the number of visitors to a particular business and thus the revenue it earns. Through our project we provide a deeper examination into Yelp's five start rating to better understand how the choice of words in reviews and a business's location can impact the rating it receives on the platform.

Key Terms¶

  • Stars: The number of stars a reviewer rated a restaraunt in a review.
  • Rural: A low population area/ town.
  • Urban: A high population area/ city.

Research Questions¶

1) How do ratings of restaurants influence positive/ negative word choice?

2) What's the distribution of ratings based on urban vs rural locations in the U.S.?

Key Findings¶

  • We found that for the first research question, people tend to use positive words more in 3, 4, and 5 star reviews than in 1 and 2 star reviews. We also found that people tend to use negative words regardless of the review type.
  • For the second research question, we found that there was no significant difference between urban and rural locations in terms of review stars.

#1: How do words under positive/ negative contexts influence ratings of restaurants?¶

We would like to examine the relationship between the number of stars in reviews for restaurants versus the amount of positive and negative words.

Hypothesis & Experiment¶

Our hypothesis is that 5 star reviews will have positive feelings associated, such as “excellent”, “love”, etc. with it. We think that 1 star reviews will have negative feelings associated with it, such as “terrible”, “hate”, etc. We believe that the “in-between ratings,” 3 stars would have words that are associated with feelings of mediocrity, 2 stars would have words with more negative feelings but some positive, 4 stars would have words that are more associated with more positive but some negative feelings.

It is generally the accepted idea that 5 stars for a business would mean that the reviewer has a positive experience and 1 star review means the opposite. 3 stars would mean that the reviewer felt it was a fair experience, perhaps not the best or the worst. We want to dive into what is the general cut off for each of the ratings; we would like to examine the distribution of the positive and negative words in the reviews of the restaurants.

We aim to do this by comparing the positive words list between the different star ratings and the negative words list between the different ratings and using a t-test to find the differences between review stars and positivity/ negativity.

The experiment would be to take the top 10 words (by frequency) that are considered as “positive” and the top 10 words (by frequency) that are considered as “negative” from the reviews dataset. Then, we will conduct text analysis to examine how many of these words appear in each review pertaining to the different star categories and calculate the proportion of each rating. We will look at on average what each proportion is.

Rationale and Real World Significance¶

This analysis may seem like common sense and obvious to some, but if we look further into the idea, people have not tested the patterns that occur within reviews. These patterns can lead to interesting conclusions about how people tend to showcase their opinions in the form of reviews. By analyzing these patterns, we can get some interesting results and hypothesize the behavior of how people interact when conducting criticism of people. Therefore, this can lead to some interesting sociological/ psychological ideas in future experiments regarding people's opinions.

If lower proportion (for 2 to 5 stars, meaning less positive words than hypothesized):

  • On average, reviewers are hyper critical to restaurant businesses and would be more likely to leave a review with more feelings of negativity despite a potentially otherwise positive experience if it were not for one incident. The dataset might be biased towards this as people tend to leave negative reviews more frequently than positive reviews.

If higher proportion (meaning more positive words than hypothesized):

  • On average, it means that people tend to be more understanding to restaurant businesses, and can leave positive criticism as an idea of being nice.

The results can make businesses be more cautious of potentially where to best manage their budget on average. Perhaps a certain word/phrase (like ‘friendly’) that frequently appears that’s associated with a higher than expected proportion of positive to negative reviews. This means that restaurants should invest more into staff members and cut down on other aspects of their businesses if they can. This experiment can be broadened to the study of human behavior where we are trying to see to what extent is the statement about people remembering one negative experience over multiple positive experiences true.

#2 What's the distribution of ratings based on urban vs rural locations?¶

We would like to see if the type of area the restaurants are located in has an effect on the distribution of ratings of the businesses.

Hypothesis & Experiment¶

We hypothesize that rural areas or less crowded locations will see on average a higher distribution of higher star (4 and 5 stars) reviews because we believe that people there tend to be more of a community and therefore, more understanding and less critical. They may have a higher tolerance for small mistakes that may cause a negative experience while people from highly crowded areas will have a lower tolerance for the same negative experience.

We believe that the average ratings of businesses in less populated areas will be higher than that of highly populated areas. We will perform this analysis by first conducting a single variable linear regression to examine if there is a relationship between the population of an area and that businesses' average ratings of the area. We will evaluate the significance of the difference between the average ratings of less populated areas and highly populated areas through a permutation test.

We will conduct this experiment through analyzing the locations of the dataset. There’s a total of 600 unique locations and we will take the 50 most populated cities and 50 of the less populated cities. We will set a limit of population count for the least populated cities because we still want a big enough sample size and some of the lesser populated cities do not have as many reviews. Afterwards, we will examine the distribution of star ratings of these two groups and test to see if there’s any differences between them.

Rationale and Real World Significance¶

We conducted this analysis with the intention to prove that people who live in urban areas are more critical than those who live in rural areas. Since urban cities have more restaraunts and places available to the average city resident, it would make sense that those who also live in those areas would be more critical to giving lower ratings. Therefore, this analysis would give insight to the general behaviors of city residents versus rural residents.

If our hypothesis turns out to be true, it means that this information will help individuals who hope to start a small business and opening a business in less crowded areas would be more likely to be successful since as an individual, the owner may not have a high budget and thus may not be able to keep up with its competitors if they were in a highly populated area. While there are benefits of opening a business in a more populated area, if our hypothesis is correct, then it makes more sense to start a business in a less populated area because the ratings of a business plays a role in the amount of business generated. Highly rated businesses would attract more customers than lowly rated businesses.

If our hypothesis turns out to be false, there’s some implications for small businesses trying to start out. If the distributions of ratings for businesses in less crowded areas lean more towards the lower side (1, 2, 3 star ratings), compared to those of very crowded areas, then potential business owners may want to think about opening a business in a more populated area despite the higher costs of operation. A study published by Harvard Business School in 2011 claimed that each "star" in a Yelp rating affected the sales of a business by 5–9 percent. Likewise, a study by some Berkeley economists in 2012 claimed that there was a 19% increase the restaurant’s bookings on Yelp during peak hours when a business’s stars increases from 3.5 to 4.5 stars.

If there's no difference, then a business owner may still consider having a business in less populated areas since this means that the business will probably see similar amount of customers based on ratings alone, when adjusted for the costs of living in the area. Of course, there are other things to consider but this result would mean that location does not have a role to play in average ratings of the business. Business owners may find it better to operate in a less crowded environment because they will be able to establish a closer tie to their consumer base due to the relatively smaller size of the area. This may generate a more loyal customer base than if the business were to be located in a larger area.

Good¶

It seems that negativity is used very generally, and that people should learn to be less negative, especially when giving a positive review, so that restaraunts can still have more confidence in their abilities.

As said throughout this analysis, the word list is a non-random, handpicked list of words thought to be good/ bad and used in reviews. Limitations¶

One thing that we must keep in mind when examining these results is that these are simply average ratings of particular locations; the average ratings are not weighted equally. This means that more highly populated areas (such as large cities/suburbs) will most likely experience more reviews on average compare to less populated areas (such as urban clusters). This affects our results greatly as demonstrated by the extreme outlier we see in the scatterplot that depicts a data point in the bottom. A potential explanation for this outlier is that there was not enough reviews, seeing how relatively small the population was.

Another thing to remember when looking at these results is the sample size. Due to the method of cleaning the data where we tried to match location names and states with population data from a different dataset, we were only able to acquire 30 unique locations. There were multiple instances where the location names and states of both datasets were the same but the way the location was written in one dataset was different, and therefore, could not be matched. We attempted to match the locations and cities manually but we were unable to accomplish that due to the size of the dataset (approximately 2 million data points). Given that the sample size is 30, it is not large enough to be certain of our results.

As noted above, our data led us to a conclusion that there was no relationship between the population of a location and the average star ratings of that particular location. However, will this be true if we were to focus in on less populated areas and highly populated areas?

Given how our data is small in size (n = 11 and n = 14), we cannot approximate the population variance from our sample variance. Interpretation¶

If we have a large p-value that exceeds a certain signifiance level or is extremely close to 1, we fail to reject the null hypothesis. If we have a low p-value that is below a certain signifiance level or is extremely close to 0, we reject the null hypothesis and accept the alternate hypothesis as the more likely outcome.

From our data, there's a large p-value. In fact, it is very close to 1. A p-value close to 0 means that the null hypothesis of no difference between the means of both samples (small cities/suburbs' and large cities/suburbs' average star ratings) is false (meaning there is a difference). A p-value close to 1, like in our case, means that the null hypothesis is more likely to be true and since our p-value is extremely close to 1, this means there's almost no difference in the means of the two samples.

Conclusion¶

From our experiment, there does not appear to be a relationship between the population size and the average ratings of businesses. This result was based on our linear regression, which had a negative r-squared value, -0.10, which we approximated to 0. This 0 r-squared value indicates that there's no linear relationship between the population of a location and the average business ratings of that location.

Furthermore, there's no difference in the average star ratings of businesses located in a less populated location versus a highly populated location. This conclusion was first discovered when examining the difference in average ratings of businesses located in large city/suburbs, aproximately 3.55043, and the average ratings of businesses located in small city/suburbs, aproximately 3.54987. This difference was around 0.00056, which is essentially zero.

To quantify the significance of this difference, we looked at a t-test of independence and permutation test. The t-test of independence resulted in a p-value that was incredibly high, 0.998. Given how close it was to 1, it is abundantly clear that we our null hypothesis, there is no difference between the two groups, is the most probable outcome. To verify this outcome, the permutation test we conducted yielded a series of random differences in means of the two groups. The difference we obtained from our data, aproximately 0.00056, was matched against a histogram that demonstrates the distribution of these random differences. Without question, the statistic of interest is well within the range of possible random differences due to its position being in the center of the distribution. Since the difference in means of our data, 0.00056, was not in either tails of the range, it means that there is no difference in the average business ratings of small and large cities/suburbs. Therefore, our prediction of there being a difference between opinions of people in urban and rural areas is false, as people from any location have the same average opinions.
